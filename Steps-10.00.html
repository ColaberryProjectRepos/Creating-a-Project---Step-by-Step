Model Reporting

Reporting on the results of your Machine Learning (ML) model is a crucial step in any ML project. It helps you and your stakeholders understand how well your model is performing and whether it meets the project's objectives. Here are different ways to report on the results of your ML model:

Choose 2 of the below 14 reporting options for your Model.  Create up to 2 ppt slides documenting your model results.  This information will submitted for an upcoming Stress Test

  1. Performance Metrics:
    • Precision, Recall, F1-Score: These metrics are commonly used for classification tasks to evaluate the model's ability to correctly classify instances.
    • Accuracy: Measures the overall correctness of predictions.
    • Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Mean Squared Error (MSE): Used for regression tasks to quantify the prediction error.
    • Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (AUC-ROC) and Area Under the Precision-Recall curve (AUC-PR): Metrics for binary classification models.
    • R-squared (R2): Measures the proportion of the variance in the target variable that the model explains for regression tasks.
  2. Confusion Matrix: A table that provides detailed information about the model's performance in classification tasks, including True Positives, True Negatives, False Positives, and False Negatives.
  3. Visualizations:
    • ROC Curve: A graphical representation of a binary classifier's performance.
    • Precision-Recall Curve: Shows the trade-off between precision and recall.
    • Calibration Plots: Assess the model's predicted probabilities against actual outcomes.
    • Feature Importance Plots: Highlight the importance of features in the model's decision-making.
  4. Business Impact:
    • Discuss how the model's performance impacts the business, such as cost savings, revenue increase, or customer satisfaction improvement.
    • Calculate return on investment (ROI) or other relevant business metrics.
  5. Interpretability:
    • Explain the model's decision-making process using techniques like feature importance analysis or SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values.
    • Create local explanations for individual predictions.
  6. Data Quality and Preprocessing:
    • Report on the quality of the dataset used for training and testing.
    • Describe the preprocessing steps applied to the data, such as normalization, encoding, or imputation.
  7. Model Selection and Hyperparameters:
    • Document the choice of algorithms and architectures used.
    • Mention the hyperparameters tuned and their values.
    • Explain the rationale behind model selection.
  8. Cross-Validation:
    • Report the results of cross-validation, if performed, to assess the model's generalization performance.
  9. Model Limitations:
    • Identify the limitations of your model, including potential biases, data limitations, and assumptions made.
  10. Recommendations:
    • Provide actionable insights based on the model's results, such as suggestions for improving model performance or data collection.
  11. A/B Testing (if applicable):
    • Describe any A/B testing or deployment strategies used to validate the model's effectiveness in a real-world scenario.
  12. Visualization of Predictions:
    • Show sample predictions and their corresponding probabilities or confidence scores.
  13. Summary and Conclusion:
    • Summarize the main findings and conclusions, highlighting both strengths and weaknesses of the model.
  14. Future Work:
    • Discuss potential avenues for future improvements, including data collection, feature engineering, or model enhancements.

The choice of reporting methods depends on the nature of your ML project, the audience, and the specific goals you aim to achieve with the model. A well-documented and transparent report helps stakeholders make informed decisions and gain confidence in the model's performance and utility.
